Audit  GTO Drill — in-game decision screen Date  2026-05-17 Type  Targeted recomposition
design audit · recomposition

The decision screen is a flat stack — nothing outranks anything, so the player reads the whole card to find the one thing the screen asks for.

A poker decision needs a focal order: see the spot, choose the move, rate the read. The current screen gives the spot, the move, the confidence rating and an optional note the same volume. This recomposition gives the screen a spine.

On fidelity. The screenshot tool is unreliable in this environment, so the screen schematics below are clean reconstructions built from the running app's rendered structure and computed styles — not pixel captures. They are accurate to layout and hierarchy, schematic on exact spacing. No source code is quoted; every recommendation is a design move, not an implementation instruction.
Telos

GTO Drill is an asynchronous two-player GTO poker quiz. Both players get the same "gotcha" hands, commit to an action, and rate their confidence 1–5; a wrap-up later surfaces the hands where they were both most sure and most disagreed.

The audited moment is the decision screen — a player sitting with one hand, deciding. (A player's turn in the async game is a handful of these decisions; "turn" here never means the poker street.) Everything on that screen should serve a single outcome: a considered decision, made in something close to a real player's mindset. The audience runs from casual learner to serious student, so the decision screen should sit at the calm, casual end — depth one tap away, never crowding the moment of choice.

Orientation — where the decision screen breaks down

  1. The spot is the only hero — and it's buried. The replay table is the strongest thing on the card but sits mid-stack, equal to the text above and the inputs below.
  2. The description re-narrates the replay. A dense paragraph says in prose what the table already shows.
  3. Three hands stack into one scroll. "Your handful" is three full cards plus a far-away submit — a wall, not a sequence.
  4. Action and confidence are styled as twins. Two near-identical button rows flatten the real priority between them.

The recomposition

The four moves

Move 01 / 4

Give the screen a spine: table → move → confidence → note.

Right now those four arrive at one volume, so the eye lands nowhere and the player parses the whole card to find the one thing the screen asks for.

Why: A poker decision has a natural order of attention — read the situation, choose the action, then register how sure you are. The screen should encode exactly that order in size and weight. The replay table becomes the visual hero at the top. The action choice is the one unmistakable primary control beneath it. Confidence drops to a quiet secondary strip. The optional note collapses to a single faint affordance. When the layout's vertical order is the decision's mental order, the player stops searching and starts deciding — which is the mindset the telos asks for.

Move 02 / 4

Cut the description — the replay already tells the spot.

The dense narration paragraph and the replay table say the same thing twice, in two formats, forcing the player to read the spot before they can see it.

Why: The replay table shows stacks, positions, blinds and the betting action far better than a prose sentence — that is the whole point of having built it. Keeping the paragraph above it means the first thing the player meets is a wall of grey text restating what the picture below will show anyway. Demote it: one short caption line under the table for orientation ("BB checks the K72 flop — your move"), and move the full reasoning out of the decision screen entirely. This is where the granularity 1–2 spectrum resolves cleanly — the casual decision surface stays lean; the deep GTO reasoning belongs to the reveal, after the player has committed, not before.

Move 03 / 4

One hand fills the screen — the handful becomes a sequence.

Three full scenario cards stacked into a single scroll is a wall: no focus, no sense of progress, and a submit button stranded far below the last one.

Why: Stacking the handful asks the player to hold three undecided hands in their head at once — the opposite of the focused, one-spot-at-a-time mindset a real decision needs. Show one hand, full-screen, with a small "Hand 1 of 3" progress marker. Commit, advance, repeat. The batch is preserved as a concept — you still build a handful before the opponent's turn — but it becomes a short, legible sequence with a clear finish line instead of an endless card. Progress markers also make the turn feel bounded and quick, which matters for an async game opened in spare moments.

Move 04 / 4

The action is the decision; confidence is a gut-check. Stop styling them as twins.

Two near-identical button rows give the action choice and the 1–5 rating equal visual rank, when one is the decision and the other is a quick read on it.

Why: Choosing the action is the hand. Rating confidence is a half-second gut-check layered on top — valuable, because it feeds the disagreement wrap-up, but secondary in the moment. Make the action options large, full-width, clearly the primary surface; let a selected one fill with the accent so the commitment is visible. Render confidence as a smaller, lighter 1–5 strip set apart below — ideally only surfacing once an action is picked, so the player meets one question at a time. Same two inputs, but the styling now tells the truth about which one is the decision.

Priority — impact per pixel

  1. One hand at a time, table as hero. The single biggest readability gain — it converts a wall into a focused decision and fixes the missing hierarchy in one move.
  2. Demote the description to a caption. Removes the duplicated grey text block; the replay carries the spot.
  3. Re-rank action over confidence. Big primary action surface, light secondary confidence strip — the styling should state the priority.
  4. Collapse the note. A faint "+ note" affordance; the textarea expands only when wanted.

What to keep

  • The replay table. It is the correct hero — this recomposition promotes it, it doesn't replace it. The build instinct was right.
  • The 1–5 confidence mechanic. The app's signature, and the engine behind the disagreement wrap-up. Keep it exactly — only lighten its visual weight on the turn.
  • The dark, poker-felt register. The recomposition sharpens it toward modern-webapp clarity; it does not trade away the green felt or the game's identity.
